Privacy Policy

Last updated: April 9, 2026

This Privacy Policy applies to DAYYVIID ENTITIES and its affiliated entities when we act as the controller of your Personal Data. In line with the Nigeria Data Protection Act (NDPA) 2023, we are committed to the transparent and lawful processing of all information entrusted to us.

Core Principle: You own your content. In accordance with the principle of consensus ad idem, we use your data only to operate the service and do not utilize it for AI training without express authorization.

1. Applicability

This statement serves as constructive notice under the NDPA 2023. It applies whenever dayyviid Entities acts as a Data Controller, specifically during service subscriptions, interactions with Support Forums, or registration for corporate events. It establishes the legal perimeter of our data processing activities within the digital ecosystem.

In the Nigerian legal system, the scope of such statements is often tested against the "Right to Privacy" under Section 37 of the 1999 Constitution. We ensure that every point of contact—from deployments to forum posts—is covered by this protective legal umbrella, preventing arbitrary interference with your digital liberty and ensuring administrative fairness.

2. Personal Data We Collect

The collection of data is strictly governed by the principle of Data Minimization. We collect direct identifiers such as names, billing addresses, and connected accounts (GitHub/GitLab) solely to fulfill our contractual obligations. This avoids "fishing expeditions" that modern regulations, including the NDPR, seek to curb in the interest of the data subject.

Automated data, including IP addresses and device identifiers, are collected for technical security and platform optimization. Citing the spirit of Medical and Dental Practitioners Disciplinary Tribunal v. Okonkwo (2001), we respect the user's right to digital self-determination by ensuring automated tracking is limited to what is strictly necessary for platform functionality and safety.

3. Purposes and Legal Basis

We process data under the legal bases of Performance of a Contract and Legitimate Interest. When a user engages our services, a binding agreement is formed, and processing becomes a mechanical necessity. Any secondary use, such as service optimization or security monitoring, is balanced against your fundamental rights to ensure no override of personal privacy occurs.

Under Nigerian Administrative Law, as seen in Director-General, SSS v. Agbakoba (1999), any interference with personal liberty must be justified by law and executed within reasonable bounds. Consequently, our processing for marketing or quality assurance is clearly defined and never exercised outside the specific purposes communicated to the data subject at the point of collection.

4. Disclosure of Personal Data

Disclosure is strictly limited to authorized Service Providers, Affiliates, and Public Authorities. Third-party processors are legally bound by confidentiality standards equivalent to our own. Disclosure to authorities only occurs when we are legally compelled by a valid subpoena or court order, ensuring the "chain of custody" for your private information remains intact and protected.

The Nigerian court in Adeyemi v. Adeyemi (2014) discussed the boundaries of private information. For dayyviid Entities, disclosing information without a valid legal basis would constitute a breach of confidence and a violation of duty. Therefore, these disclosures are not discretionary but are guided by stringent legal frameworks to protect the user's inherent interests.

5. International Transfers

To ensure protection travels with the data across borders, we utilize Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs) and recognized Data Privacy Frameworks. This prevents the "exportation" of data to jurisdictions with weak privacy laws, ensuring that Nigerian users' data is not lost in a "legal black hole" once it leaves local servers for global processing centers.

While the principle of Extra-territoriality is nuanced in Nigerian law, the NDPA creates a bridge for international data flow. We ensure that any receiving country maintains an "Adequacy Decision" level of protection, maintaining our accountability regardless of the physical location of the data storage facility or the nationality of the processor.

6. Children’s Privacy

In accordance with the Child Rights Act, we do not knowingly collect data from individuals under 16. In the Nigerian legal context, a minor generally lacks the capacity to enter into a binding contract. Since our services are contractual in nature, any account held by a minor is effectively voidable to protect the minor's interests from legal entanglement.

By terminating accounts suspected of being owned by minors, we fulfill our administrative duty to act in the "Best Interest of the Child." This proactive measure prevents the exploitation of those who cannot legally consent to complex data processing terms, shielding both the entity and the minor from the legal complications of unauthorized data harvesting.

7. Data Retention

Data is retained only for the duration necessary for the original purpose or legal compliance. For instance, financial records are kept to satisfy tax statutes, while session metadata is purged promptly. Once the "Life Cycle" of the data expires, it is permanently deleted or rendered anonymous to prevent re-identification through data lingering.

The principle of Laches and Acquiescence suggests that rights must be exercised promptly; similarly, holding data longer than necessary creates a "storage liability." We follow the administrative rule of "Reasonableness," keeping only what is required for operations while respecting the user's right to be forgotten once the contractual relationship ends.

8. Your Rights

Users are endowed with "Digital Human Rights," including access, rectification, and erasure. The Right to Portability allows you to move your data between services, promoting competition. These rights are the digital counterparts to the "Fundamental Rights" found in Chapter IV of the Nigerian Constitution, tailored for the information age.

In Uzoukwu v. Ezeonu II (1991), the court noted that fundamental rights are not mere window dressing. Similarly, your rights to object to or restrict processing are functional tools that provide agency over your digital identity. We view our users not as mere data points, but as participants in a legal relationship with enforceable claims against the controller.

9. How to Exercise Your Rights

Requests are processed within one month, reflecting the administrative principle of Expeditiousness. This section serves as your "Procedural Manual" for seeking redress. If a request is denied, we provide reasons in adherence to the principle of Natural Justice and the rule of audi alteram partem (hear the other side).

Transparency ensures the relationship between dayyviid Entities and the user remains balanced. By maintaining a clear channel for rights exercise, we remain accountable for our data decisions and provide a forum for internal resolution before the need for formal litigation or regulatory intervention arises.

10. Security Measures

We implement technical and organizational measures, such as encryption and multi-factor authentication, to prevent unauthorized access. In the law of torts, a failure to maintain these "Technical Guardrails" could be viewed as Negligence, especially if we fail to meet the standard of a "reasonable" tech entity in the face of foreseeable risks.

The case of Anyah v. Imo Concorde Hotels Ltd (2002) emphasizes that the standard of care is determined by circumstances. In our case, the "circumstances" involve the evolving nature of cyber threats, necessitating that our security measures remain dynamic and proactive rather than static, ensuring the fortress of your data remains secure.

11. Third-Party Links

Our website may contain links to external sites not governed by this policy. Once you leave our domain, you enter a separate "Legal Jurisdiction." This is a Caveat Emptor (User Beware) scenario where we cannot be held liable for the privacy practices or data breaches of external third parties beyond our control.

This serves as a Limitation of Liability clause, defining the boundaries of our responsibility. In Nigerian law, parties are generally bound by the terms they agree to; by notifying you of this boundary, we limit our "Duty of Care" to the services we directly control and the data we directly manage.

12. Changes to This Statement

We reserve the right to update this statement to reflect changes in law or technology. Significant changes will be notified via our website, ensuring the contract remains transparent. This aligns with the principle that administrative policies should be prospective, ensuring no "Surprise Terms" are enforced against the user retroactively.

In Lakanmi v. Attorney General of Western Nigeria (1970), the court frowned upon arbitrary changes to legal status. While this is a private policy, the same logic applies: users have a "Legitimate Expectation" that their data will not be handled in a way they never originally agreed to, preserving the sanctity of the agreement.

13. Data Privacy Framework

The DPF is a mechanism for transfers between specific global jurisdictions, signifying our certification to maintain high data protection standards. This is a form of International Regulatory Compliance, acting as a badge of "Good Standing" in the global digital community and ensuring seamless transatlantic data flows.

For a Nigerian entity, observing these frameworks represents "Global Best Practice." Adopting these rigors ensures we are prepared for international scrutiny and meet global expectations for data excellence, even when operating across diverse regulatory landscapes that require varying levels of technical and legal compliance.

14. Regional Supplements

Specific disclosures are provided for regions like California (CCPA). These supplements offer additional protections, such as the right to opt-out of data "sales." These regional laws often influence our global "Privacy-First" culture, setting a high bar for all users regardless of their specific geographical location.

In Nigerian legal practice, we often look to persuasive authorities from other jurisdictions. Similarly, the protections mandated by the CCPA or GDPR influence the "Standard of Care" we apply globally, ensuring all users benefit from advanced privacy protections that reflect the most modern legal thoughts on data rights.

15. Cookie Policy

We distinguish between Essential Cookies (required for site operation) and Non-Essential Cookies (used for marketing). In line with the principle of Informed Consent, users are provided with the choice to accept or reject tracking technologies that are not strictly necessary for the site to function.

This is rooted in the concept of Autonomy. The NDPR emphasizes that consent must be "freely given, specific, and informed." Our cookie management system is the digital manifestation of this legal requirement, ensuring transparency in how your browsing data is utilized for analytics or advertising purposes.

dayyviid Entities operates through various subsidiaries that may access data for operational purposes. While each entity is a separate "Legal Personality"—per Salomon v. Salomon—they function as a single economic unit to ensure seamless service delivery and technical support to our global user base.

In Nigerian law, as seen in Marina Nominees Ltd. v. FBIR (1986), the corporate veil can be examined to ensure accountability. By disclosing our affiliates, we remain transparent about our corporate structure, ensuring you know exactly which entities are responsible for your information within our organizational hierarchy.
